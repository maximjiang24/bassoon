Bassoon Intonation Analyzer

Record audio (and optionally webcam video) to analyze pitch stability and embouchure consistency

How to use this tool

Getting started

Record tab — Click Start Recording while you play your bassoon. The app listens through your microphone and measures how steadily you hold your pitch.

Upload tab — Already have a WAV file? Upload it here to analyze a previous recording.

Track embouchure with webcam — Check this box if you also want to record your face. The camera watches your lips, mouth, and jaw and checks whether your facial technique is affecting your pitch.

Summary numbers

Rating
An overall grade — Excellent, Good, Fair, or Needs work — based on how steady your pitch was throughout the recording.
Stability score
A number from 0 to 1. Closer to 1.0 = very steady pitch. Below 0.4 means noticeable wobble. Think of it like a percentage of steadiness.
Mean pitch
The average note you were playing — shown as a note name like "A4" and its frequency in Hz. This tells you what you played, not how well.
Mean bias
How far your pitch drifted from perfectly in tune, measured in cents (100 cents = one semitone). +10¢ means slightly sharp (too high). −10¢ means flat (too low). Anything within ±10¢ is generally fine.
Tendency
Whether you drift Sharp (too high), Flat (too low), or stay Centered. Useful for identifying a consistent habit.
Voiced %
What percentage of the recording had an actual musical note in it. If this is low, try playing louder or closer to the microphone.
Unstable regions
The number of short moments where your pitch wobbled significantly. Zero is ideal.
Embouchure (if webcam used)
A score from 0 to 1 showing how consistent your mouth shape was. Higher is better.
Overall |r| (if webcam used)
How strongly your embouchure movements overall connect to your pitch changes. Closer to 1 = your technique directly affects your intonation.

Graphs

Pitch Contour
A line showing how your pitch moved up and down over time. A flat, horizontal line is ideal. Big swings or drift mean unstable intonation. The dashed line in the middle is perfectly in tune.
Stability Heatmap
Each colored block is one second of playing. Green = steady. Red = wobbly. Grey means silence. Good playing looks mostly green. Look for patterns — do red blocks cluster at the start, end, or on certain notes?
Register Comparison
Your bassoon has three pitch ranges: Low (very low notes), Tenor (middle, most common), and High (upper notes). This bar chart compares how stable you are in each range. A short bar in one register means that range needs work.
Embouchure Timeline
Only shown when you use the webcam. Four lines track your mouth width, mouth height, jaw position, and lip tension over time. Flat, steady lines = consistent technique. Sudden jumps mean your embouchure is changing mid-phrase.
Jaw vs Pitch
Only shown when you use the webcam. This overlays your jaw movement on your pitch changes. If the two lines move together (rise and fall at the same times), your jaw is directly influencing your pitch — worth addressing in practice.
Correlation Heatmap
Only shown when you use the webcam. Shows how strongly each part of your embouchure connects to your pitch stability. Dark blue = moving together (positive link). Dark red = moving opposite (negative link). White/grey = no connection. The number shown is the Pearson r value (−1 to +1).

Embouchure analysis (webcam only)

Consistency score
How steady your overall mouth shape was. 0.85+ = excellent, 0.65+ = good, below 0.4 = inconsistent.
Jaw stability
How still your jaw was. 1.0 = perfectly still. Lower values mean your jaw moved around while playing.
Correlation table (r column)
The r value runs from −1 to +1. Close to +1 or −1 = strong connection between that movement and your pitch. Close to 0 = no connection. A * means the result is statistically significant (not just random chance).
Interpretation
Plain-English takeaways automatically generated from your results. These are the most actionable lines — start here when deciding what to work on.

Unstable regions table

Each row is a specific moment where your pitch wobbled most. The Note column shows which note you were playing — useful for identifying which notes consistently give you trouble.

Register breakdown table

Shows your stability score and number of wobbly moments for each of the three bassoon registers. If your High register score is much lower than Tenor, focus your practice on upper-register tone control.

Tips for better results

  • Play long tones for the clearest data — short notes are harder to analyze.
  • Record in a quiet room; background noise lowers Voiced %.
  • If using the webcam, sit still and face the camera directly so your face is visible the whole time.
  • Try recording the same passage before and after focused practice to track improvement.
